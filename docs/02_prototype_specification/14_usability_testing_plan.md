## 14. Usability-Testing Plan

### 14.1 Participants

Recruit representative international travelers, prioritizing ages 35–55 while including the broader 30–65 range. Cover at least couples/honeymooners, families, first-time Greece visitors, and time-poor premium travelers. Include mobile-first users and participants with relevant accessibility needs where feasible. Avoid relying only on travel-industry or internal participants.

### 14.2 Test approach

Use moderated task-based sessions for the first prototype round. Ask participants to think aloud, but evaluate behavior and comprehension rather than stated visual preference alone. Do not explain the product before the opening-screen comprehension task.

### 14.3 Core scenarios

1. **Offer comprehension:** From Home, explain what Greek Essence does, who it is for, and what action you would take next.
2. **Destination choice:** Plan a first Greece trip combining city and island; find a suitable destination and explain why it fits.
3. **Authenticity/trust:** Decide whether the company feels credible and identify the evidence used.
4. **Journey interpretation:** Review a sample journey and explain whether it is fixed/bookable or customizable.
5. **Family form:** Start a request for two adults and children, enter preferences, and move backward/forward without losing data.
6. **Budget comprehension:** Explain what the budget field asks, why it is needed, and whether flights are included based on prototype copy.
7. **Error recovery:** Correct an invalid field and recover from a simulated submission failure.
8. **Next-step comprehension:** After confirmation, explain what will happen and how to amend information.
9. **Secondary contact:** Find a way to ask a general question without completing the trip request.
10. **Language continuity:** Switch language from a detail page and determine whether context was preserved.

### 14.4 Measures and observations

Record:

- accurate offer and audience comprehension;
- ability to distinguish planning service from booking/package marketplace;
- task completion and wrong turns;
- time/effort and confidence at key decisions;
- CTA interpretation;
- trust evidence recalled and any misleading attribution;
- destination/route fit comprehension;
- form abandonment points, hesitation, validation issues, and privacy concerns;
- understanding of customization and next steps;
- mobile target, menu, and text readability issues;
- language-switch expectations.

### 14.5 Prototype-level success signals

- Most participants accurately describe the service after the opening screen and first section.
- No participant reasonably interprets journeys as live fixed packages after viewing a detail page.
- Participants find `Plan My Trip` without prompting but do not perceive it as aggressive.
- Representative users complete the four-step form and recover from the designed error without data loss.
- Participants can name at least two credible reasons to trust the service without relying on invented reviews.
- Participants correctly explain the next step after submission.

Numeric pass thresholds should be set with the research sample size and are not invented in this document.
