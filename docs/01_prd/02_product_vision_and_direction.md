## 2. Product Vision and Direction

### 2.1 Vision

Greek Essence should become the trusted digital starting point for travelers who want to experience Greece beyond standard tourism without carrying the full burden of research, coordination, and risk themselves.

The initial product is intentionally focused. It should solve today's validated problem well: communicate the offer, inspire confidence, qualify serious travelers, and enable a reliable human follow-up process.

The long-term direction is evolutionary rather than predictive. The product may eventually become a full-stack travel platform with authentication, client-facing tools, workflow automation, itinerary management, payments, integrations, and operational features. Those possibilities should remain feasible, but they must not be prematurely implemented or allowed to distort the MVP.

### 2.2 Architectural Product Philosophy

The product should be organized so that each meaningful iteration makes it more stable, mature, understandable, and maintainable. Future growth should occur through deliberate, validated additions rather than accumulating unrelated features or fragile shortcuts.

The project should prefer current, popular, well-maintained, and battle-tested practices over experimental novelty. Specific technologies belong in the Technical Design and should be selected based on the needs and constraints known at implementation time.

### 2.3 Product Horizon

The PRD distinguishes three horizons:

1. **MVP validation:** marketing site, content, trust, custom-trip request, manual follow-up.
2. **Operational optimization:** improved content, analytics, lead management, CRM or itinerary-platform integration, workflow automation.
3. **Platform evolution:** authenticated client experiences, trip-management tools, payments, richer integrations, or other features justified by validated business needs.

No fixed five-year feature roadmap is required. Long-range features must be re-evaluated when evidence and business conditions exist.

### Key Decisions

- Build today's product without unnecessarily restricting tomorrow's product.
- Do not speculate deeply about distant features.
- Future capabilities are hypotheses, not commitments.
- Product maturity and maintainability are explicit long-term goals.
