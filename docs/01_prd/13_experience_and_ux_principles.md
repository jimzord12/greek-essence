# Part III — Product Experience

## 13. Experience and UX Principles

### 13.1 Experience Goal

The site should feel like meeting a knowledgeable, cultivated Greek host who listens carefully, understands international expectations, knows both famous locations and hidden gems, and handles details with taste and discretion.

### 13.2 UX Principles

1. **Inspire before selling.** Lead with place, emotion, and possibility.
2. **Make expertise visible.** Show local knowledge, hospitality experience, partner access, and process clarity.
3. **Reduce planning uncertainty.** Help users understand fit, routes, and next steps.
4. **Qualify without unnecessary friction.** Collect enough information through a concise, well-paced journey.
5. **Use quiet premium cues.** Premium quality comes from clarity, content, composition, and restraint.
6. **Keep human support close.** Personal help remains accessible but not intrusive.
7. **Design for future growth.** Content and routing should support later expansion.
8. **Respect user attention.** No component should exist only to decorate, distract, or inflate page length.

### 13.3 UX Anti-Patterns

Avoid:

- booking-platform layouts with dense filters, prices, and availability widgets;
- promotion-heavy banners, discounts, countdowns, and aggressive pop-ups;
- long, unstructured forms;
- generic stock imagery and stereotypical Greek clichés;
- multiple CTAs competing with equal visual weight;
- excessive animation, parallax, or decorative effects;
- technical, corporate, or impersonal copy;
- price-first messaging that attracts poor-fit demand;
- misleading urgency, scarcity, pricing, availability, or social proof.

### Key Decisions

- The site prioritizes confidence and orientation over transactional density.
- Every interaction must support understanding, trust, exploration, or conversion.
